Grit: The Power of Passion and Perseverance

This blog post discusses the concept of grit by Angela Lee Duckworth, sourced from a video titled Grit: The Power of Passion and Perseverance from the YouTube channel Talks at Google.

About Angela Lee Duckworth

Angela Duckworth is a professor of psychology at the University of Pennsylvania. She is also the founder and scientific director of the Character Lab, a nonprofit organization dedicated to advancing the science and practice of character development. In 2013, Angela received the prestigious MacArthur Fellowship for her research on grit, self-control, and other non-IQ competencies that are indicative of success in life. Her accomplishments and contributions are truly impressive.

Before her career in research, Angela established a summer school for low-income children, which garnered attention and was featured as a case study at the Harvard Kennedy School. She has also worked as a management consultant at McKinsey, as well as a math and science teacher in public schools across cities like New York City, San Francisco, and Philadelphia.

Angela holds degrees in neuroscience and psychology from Harvard, Oxford, and the University of Pennsylvania. Its worth mentioning once again that she was awarded the MacArthur Fellowship in 2013. Lastly, Grit is her first book.

The Power of Grit, Deliberate Practice, and Talent: Keys to Success and Achievement

The main message conveyed in the talkshow is that the power of passion and perseverance, commonly referred to as grit, is crucial for success and achievement. Angela Duckworth argues that these qualities are at least as important as IQ and talent, which have traditionally been given more emphasis in society. Ben Fried, representing Google, questions whether organizations, including Google, focus too much on measuring innate talent in their hiring processes and suggests that they should also consider grit and fit.

Angela Duckworth acknowledges the significance of talent and potential but emphasizes that the definition of talent can vary. She defines talent as the rate at which individuals increase their skills with effort. While it is legitimate for Google to seek out talented individuals, Duckworths research shows that being talented does not guarantee consistent performance or the ability to finish what one starts. She highlights the importance of daily discipline and continuous improvement, stating that it is often the individuals who put in hours of unglamorous work and demonstrate perseverance that achieve excellence.

The continuation of the conversation delves into the concept of deliberate practice and the difference between directed practice and undirected practice. Angela Duckworth explains that deliberate practice, as coined by Anders Ericsson, has four key elements:

  • Intentionality: Deliberate practice involves setting specific and precise goals, focusing on a particular area of improvement, often targeting weaknesses rather than strengths.
  • 100% Focus: It requires full concentration and effort. Duckworth mentions Pete Carroll, the coach of the Seattle Seahawks, who emphasizes practicing with great effort.
  • Feedback: Feedback is crucial in deliberate practice. Ideally, it should be immediate and rich in information, enabling individuals to adjust and refine their performance.
  • Reflection and refinement: After receiving feedback, individuals should reflect on their performance and make necessary adjustments. They repeat the practice with the intention of improving.

Duckworth uses the example of running to illustrate the concept. She describes how she had been running regularly but didnt see any improvement in her speed. When discussing this with Anders Ericsson, he asked her about her approach to running. She admitted that she didnt set specific goals, didnt concentrate on her running during the activity, didnt track her progress or seek feedback, and didnt reflect on how to refine her performance. Ericsson concluded that her running practice lacked deliberate practice, explaining why she didnt improve despite spending thousands of hours running.

The Role of Deliberate Practice and Leadership in Meaningful Improvement and Professional Development

Practice alone is not enough for improvement. Deliberate practice, which involves intentionality, focus, feedback, and reflection, is the key to achieving meaningful progress in any endeavor. Merely going through the motions or putting in effort without these deliberate elements is unlikely to lead to significant improvement.

In response to Ben Frieds question about the role of managers in helping people develop and the concept of professional development in organizations, Angela Duckworth emphasizes the importance of continuous growth and the role of leaders as parents in bringing forth the potential of their team members.

Duckworth mentions that great leaders model the character they want others to emulate. She believes that leaders do matter and have a significant impact on the organizations culture. The way leaders interact with people, including those who are not in positions of power, sets an example for others. Duckworth emphasizes that leaders should be respectful and treat everyone with kindness, as their actions are being observed and emulated by those they lead.

She also highlights the importance of leaders providing both challenge and support. Leaders should demand excellence, push their team members to do better, and provide constructive feedback. However, this challenging aspect should be balanced with genuine care and respect for the individuals success and well-being.

Embracing Failure, Demystifying Excellence, and the Challenge of Measuring Grit and Talent

The conversation then shifts to mentoring and parenting. Duckworth confirms that she shares negative peer reviews and grant proposal rejections with the people in her lab and her students. She believes in demystifying excellence and showcasing that failure is a common part of the journey towards success. By sharing these experiences, she exposes the imperfections and challenges that eventually lead to achievement. Duckworth wants her team to know the truth rather than falling into the trap of perceiving success as a shiny, polished myth.

Overall, Duckworth emphasizes the importance of leaders in modeling desired behaviors, providing both challenge and support, and embracing failure as a learning opportunity. By creating a culture that values growth and acknowledges the reality of setbacks, leaders can foster an environment conducive to personal and professional development within organizations.

Angela Duckworth acknowledges that measuring grit is more challenging compared to measuring intelligence. IQ tests have been refined over a century and provide a relatively objective and non-fakeable measure of intelligence. On the other hand, Duckworths grit scale questionnaire is susceptible to faking and subjectivity. People can easily manipulate their answers to present themselves as hard workers or gritty individuals. Additionally, the frame of reference bias can affect how individuals compare themselves to others, leading to distorted scores.

Duckworth cautions against using grit scale scores for high-stakes decisions in areas such as employment or school admissions. While researchers can account for biases and errors in statistical analysis, it is important not to rely solely on grit scores for making important judgments. The grit scale was not designed to be used as a definitive measure for hiring or admissions decisions.

As for measuring talent in computer science, Ben Fried suggests that there are well-established methods for assessing talent in that domain. However, the conversation does not delve further into the specific methods or metrics used to measure talent in computer science.

Exploring Grit-Based Hiring Assessments and Cultural Identity at Google

Angela shares her ideas about hiring and assessing individuals based on grit, a quality she believes is essential for success. She suggests looking for evidence of grit in a persons resume, such as progression, continuity, and perseverance in their previous endeavors. On the other hand, she advises against relying solely on interviews or using the grit scale for assessment.

Angela proposes a novel idea that hasnt been tested yet, which involves setting up mini grit experiments as part of the assessment process. By giving candidates challenging tasks and observing how they perform, learn, and persist, employers can gauge their level of grit firsthand. This approach aims to assess grit directly rather than trying to infer it through interviews or other means.

During their conversation, Angela also discusses the use of the term Googler and its significance. She highlights that strong cultural identities within organizations often lead to individuals identifying themselves with a noun, such as Googler, rather than just an adjective. Angela draws parallels with other examples like West Pointers and Seahawks, where a distinct culture and language create a sense of belonging and identity. She suggests that the term Googler reflects the unique culture and language of the company, emphasizing its strong cultural identity.

Additionally, Ben mentions the term Googly, which is an adjective used within the company. Angela jokingly remarks that Googly sounds more like the movement of small eyeballs and suggests sticking with the noun form, Googler, for a stronger cultural identity.

Its important to note that this conversation captures a specific moment in time and may not reflect the current views or practices at Google or Angela Duckworths most recent thoughts on the topic.

Exploring Cultural Identity, Grit, and Cross-Cultural Comparisons

Ben Fried and Angela Duckworth then discuss the connection between cultural identity and grit, as well as the potential variations in grit across different cultures or immigrant groups.

Angela mentions the Finnish concept of sisu, which roughly translates to grit. She explains that being Finnish entails having an identity where individuals tap into their inner strength and perseverance when faced with challenges. Angela highlights that cultural identities within organizations, such as being a West Pointer or a Googler, can foster a sense of collective grit and contribute to individuals willingness to persevere in difficult situations.

Ben then raises the question of whether certain cultures or immigrant groups might be grittier than others. Angela acknowledges the possibility but admits that she hasnt directly examined this question. She emphasizes that cultural differences exist and that it would be naive to claim that all cultures are the same in terms of grit.

Angela explains the challenges in comparing grit across different countries due to cultural standards and norms. She shares a study conducted in over 60 countries where people filled out personality questionnaires, including a scale of conscientiousness. Surprisingly, the data indicated that Japan, Korea, and China ranked among the least gritty areas in terms of conscientiousness. Angela suggests that these findings may be influenced by the high standards present in those countries, making it difficult to draw direct comparisons.

In conclusion, Angela emphasizes that making cross-cultural comparisons of grit is challenging due to variations in cultural standards and self-assessment within different societies.

Exploring Grit Comparisons Across Cultures and Finding Purpose in the Real World

Angela then addresses questions from the audience. The first question asks about using peer assessments across cultural or ethnic boundaries to compare grit levels. Angela explains that peer assessments might not provide an accurate baseline for grit comparison because peers within a particular culture would still be influenced by that cultures standards. She suggests using multiple sources of data, such as peer ratings, teacher ratings, performance tasks, and resumes, to triangulate and obtain a more comprehensive understanding of an individuals grit.

Another audience member compliments Angela on her audiobook and asks a question related to the challenge of choosing what to be gritty about in the real world. They mention that its easier to be gritty in college or school when there are clear metrics and goals. However, after graduating, they and their friends struggle to determine what to focus their grit on, especially when the cost of choosing incorrectly can be high. Angela acknowledges the difficulty and shares her advice.

She suggests that gritty, passionate people have two sources of motivation: interests and purpose. Regarding interests, Angela advises reflecting on the things one started enjoying around the age of 12 or 13, as those early adolescent interests can provide clues to long-term passions. Purpose, on the other hand, relates to the greater mission or values that an individual deeply cares about. By exploring the overlap between interests and purpose, individuals can gain insight into what they may want to be gritty about. Angela also mentions the importance of considering practicality and making a living, but she highlights that starting with interests and purpose is more helpful than simply following a vague notion of passion.

Overall, Angela encourages individuals to reflect on their genuine interests and core values as they navigate the process of deciding what to be gritty about in the real world.

Balancing Persistence and Knowing When to Quit: Lessons from Dating in the Corporate World

Another audience member then asks Angela Duckworth for advice on how to combine knowing when to fail and when to persist in the corporate world. Angela responds by using the metaphor of dating.

She explains that in personal life, quitting or breaking up with someone can be seen as a form of quitting. However, when it comes to dating, people often quit in search of finding their life partner. Similarly, in careers, individuals can explore different paths and make choices based on their intentions. Angela shares her own experience of quitting various jobs like the White House speech writing gig and McKinsey, not because she wanted to quit indefinitely, but because she was searching for something she would never want to quit.

She emphasizes the importance of quitting with the purpose of finding something worth sticking with. The process may involve exploration and groping toward the right path. Angela acknowledges that the journey to finding a career or a passion is not always predictable or efficient. Therefore, she suggests that quitting can be valuable when it serves the purpose of ultimately finding something one would never want to quit.

Overall, Angela encourages individuals to be intentional in their exploration, knowing that quitting can be a means to an end rather than an end in itself.

The Development of Grittiness: Moving Beyond Natural Talent and Embracing Deliberate Practice

Another audience member asks Angela for recommendations on how people can increase their grittiness. Angela responds by addressing the common notion that grit is something intrinsic or unlearnable.

She begins by urging the audience to be mindful of their language, particularly their use of the word just when describing someone who is naturally talented or skilled. Angela explains that when we use terms like just or natural, it often implies that we cant explain someones ability or achievement. She also mentions that the idea of just do it is unhelpful because it doesnt provide practical guidance.

Angela then shares her personal experience of giving talks and receiving compliments about being a natural, despite having put in countless hours of practice and refinement. She emphasizes that becoming successful at something is not a result of just anything but rather the accumulation of hours and hours of deliberate practice and iteration with feedback.

Furthermore, Angela discusses the conditions that contribute to grittiness, particularly in children. She acknowledges that genetics play a role, as children inherit traits from their parents. Additionally, parents and other role models, such as teachers or coaches, can shape a childs work ethic and passion through their own modeling behavior.

Angela also highlights the importance of virtuous cycles, where individuals find a domain they are passionate about and experience success and encouragement. This positive feedback loop fuels motivation, effort, and further achievement. Conversely, she mentions the existence of negative spirals, where individuals become discouraged, stop trying, and reinforce their own negative beliefs.

The audience member then shares their personal story of overcoming adversity as a Colombian immigrant and asks about the conditions that made them different from their peers who did not achieve similar success. Angela acknowledges that her lifes work is to answer such questions and explains that grit often results from a combination of genetic factors, modeling behavior, and virtuous cycles.

She suggests that compounding small amounts of grit, effort, and success over time may be the key to understanding why some individuals thrive while others do not. Angela expresses her desire for collaboration with organizations like Google to delve deeper into this topic.

The Multidimensional Nature of Academic Success and Embracing Uncertainty in Pursuits

Angela Duckworth discusses a study published in The Journal of Personality and Social Psychology that examined the relationship between grit, intelligence, conscientiousness, and academic success. The study focused on 4,000 UK students and found that while grit did predict standardized test scores, it was not as strong of a predictor as measures of conscientiousness and intelligence. Angela Duckworth acknowledges these findings and does not question them.

However, she emphasizes that equating standardized test scores with overall success or academic achievement can be limiting. She mentions that grades, extracurricular activities, and personal goals are important factors that are not captured by test scores alone. Angela Duckworths complaint is not with the studys findings themselves but with the idea that test scores provide a complete understanding of success for students.

Regarding the live question from the audience, the person asks what to do if they apply their grit to a pursuit but realize they may be applying it to the wrong thing or if they should switch their focus to something else where they might be more successful. Angela Duckworth responds by acknowledging the risks involved in pursuing a passion or persevering in a certain direction. She mentions that taking risks is inherent in life, and even not taking action carries its own risks. While there is a chance of ending up somewhere unintended, she argues that not being passionate and persevering guarantees failure. She suggests that grit involves putting one foot in front of the other and moving forward, even in the face of uncertainty.

Exploring the Resemblance between Grit and Obsession, and Broadening the Understanding of Achievement

In response to another question about the relationship between grit and obsession, Angela Duckworth mentions the strong resemblance between the two. She cites Temple Grandin, a renowned autistic individual, who said, A little bit of obsession gets a hell of a lot of work done. Angela Duckworth admits her own obsession with grit, where she constantly thinks about it and finds it difficult to stop pondering its implications. However, she differentiates gritty peoples obsessions from typical obsessions by highlighting that gritty individuals love what they do and wouldnt trade their lives for anything else.

Overall, Angela Duckworth acknowledges the importance of conscientiousness and intelligence in predicting academic success but emphasizes that grit should not be seen as the sole determinant of achievement. She encourages considering a broader range of factors and goals when assessing success.

The Underdog Mentality: Exploring Grit and the Ill Show You Response

In response to the audience question about the examples of individuals with great grit being underdogs who eventually succeed, Angela Duckworth acknowledges the presence of underdog psychology in many gritty individuals she has studied. While its not a requirement to be an underdog to develop grit, she finds the psychology of underdogs fascinating.

Angela Duckworth describes a characteristic she refers to as the Ill show you rebel mentality. Its a response characterized by a sense of aggression and determination when faced with skepticism or doubt from others. She shares her personal experience of feeling a hot anger when advised to withdraw from a challenging neurobiology course in college. Instead of giving up, she declared her major in neurobiology that very day. She finds it intriguing that individuals can have different psychological reactions to the same experience, with some feeling like losers and giving up while others exhibit the Ill show you response.

Angela Duckworth clarifies that while being an underdog is not necessary for developing grit, it is a common trait observed in many gritty individuals.

Summary

Grit refers to the power of passion and perseverance in achieving success. Angela Duckworth, a psychology professor and founder of the Character Lab, argues that grit is as important as IQ and talent, challenging the emphasis society traditionally places on innate abilities. She emphasizes that daily discipline, deliberate practice, and continuous improvement are crucial for excellence.

Deliberate practice involves setting specific goals, focusing intensely, seeking feedback, and reflecting on performance to make necessary adjustments. Duckworth highlights the importance of leaders in fostering growth, modeling desired behaviors, and providing both challenge and support.

The challenges of measuring grit and talent cautioning against relying solely on grit scale scores for high-stakes decisions. Angela proposes the idea of incorporating mini grit experiments into the assessment process to evaluate grit directly.

Cultural identity and its connection to grit are explored, with examples such as being a Googler or a West Pointer. Angela acknowledges the variations in grit across cultures but cautions against making direct comparisons due to differing cultural standards and self-assessment biases.

Angela also delves about into finding purpose in the real world, balancing persistence and knowing when to quit, and the development of grittiness. Angela encourages individuals to reflect on their genuine interests and core values and emphasizes that grit can be developed through deliberate practice and virtuous cycles of effort and achievement.

Overall, grit is important in achieving success and provides insights into its nature, development, and assessment.
